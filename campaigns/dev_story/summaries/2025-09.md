# September 2025: The Orchestration Engine Takes Shape

September was the month CodeMySpec stopped being a collection of parts and started becoming a machine. Across 19 commits, the project underwent a fundamental transformation -- from a system that could analyze code to one that could orchestrate multi-step design sessions driven by AI agents.

The month opened with structural groundwork. On September 1st, components gained a hierarchical model: a new `parent_component_id` field and a dedicated `HierarchicalTree` builder arrived in a pair of commits that gave the system an understanding of how components nest and depend on each other. The tree builder alone was nearly 500 lines of code and tests, signaling the ambition of the work ahead. Over the next two days, the hierarchy became operational -- a `HierarchicalChecker` enforced requirement ordering, components were scoped to accounts, and priority-based sorting gave the project coordinator the ability to decide what to build next.

Then came the scheduler. A LiveView-powered drag-and-drop interface landed on September 3rd, complete with Sortable.js integration and 230 lines of tests. It was a brief but telling detour into the frontend -- a moment where the developer paused the backend march to give the system a visual face.

But the real story of September was the orchestration pipeline. On September 22nd, a massive commit titled "The whole orchestration and session execution pipeline" landed -- 47 files changed, nearly 1,500 lines added. It introduced context design sessions with their own orchestrator, step behaviour contracts, result handlers, a sessions controller, and the full request-response lifecycle through a Phoenix API. This was the architectural spine: a generic session framework where AI-driven workflows could be defined as a sequence of typed steps (initialize, generate, validate, finalize), each returning structured results.

The pattern proved its worth immediately. By September 28th, a second session type -- component design sessions -- was built on the same foundation, reusing the orchestrator pattern and step behaviours. The CLI recorder infrastructure was rebuilt from scratch to support replay-based testing, and a `RecordingEnvironment` was introduced so that agent interactions could be captured and verified deterministically. This was not just feature work; it was the team investing in the ability to test AI-driven workflows without calling the AI.

The final days of the month were devoted to refinement. Step behaviour semantics were tightened so that every step dealt in explicit `Result` structs. A `StructIntrospector` and `FieldDescriptionRegistry` arrived to let the system self-document its own data structures -- a move toward generating specifications from code rather than just code from specifications. Validation logic was cleaned, unique constraints were fixed, and prompt instructions were clarified.

The pace told its own story: quiet, focused days of one or two commits punctuated by explosive sessions where entire subsystems materialized. September ended with CodeMySpec possessing something it had lacked at the start of the month -- a repeatable, testable, extensible engine for turning design intent into structured AI conversations. The foundation was set for everything that would follow.
